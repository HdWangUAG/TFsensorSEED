## Claim / finding (1–3 bullets, with the numbers)
- SRTF1, a **predicted TetR-family** transcription factor from *Pimelobacter simplex*, is an allosteric steroid-responsive aTF that binds a 22 bp palindromic DNA site in the absence of sterol and rapidly unbinds upon progesterone exposure: **81% dissociation at 30 s vs 4.5% in vehicle**. 〔src: "a predicted TetR-family TF in a divergent promoter with a predicted ketosteroid-dehydrogenase gene"〕 〔src: "SRTF1 rapidly dissociated after exposure to progesterone, with 81% dissociation at 30 s after exposure compared to 4.5% in vehicle (Fig. 2d, e)."〕
- Dissociation is **highly progesterone-specific**: no response to cholesterol, β-estradiol, or estrone; only **10% (aldosterone)** and **13% (cortisol)** dissociation at 30 s; no response to the urine metabolites pregnanediol or pregnanediol-glucuronide. 〔src: "Exposure to cholesterol or the estrogens beta-estradiol or estrone resulted in no SRTF1 dissociation relative to control, while exposure to aldosterone or cortisol yielded only 10 and 13% dissociation, respectively (Fig. 2d, e)."〕 〔src: "no differences in dissociation were observed after exposure to 5β-Pregnane-3α,20-α-diol (pregnanediol) and 5β-Pregnane-3α,20-α-diol-glucuronide (pregnanediol-glucuronide)"〕
- A **two-mutation variant SRTF1_MUT1** (mutations in a putative steroid-binding region) gave a **6-fold decrease in cortisol cross-reactivity (to 5.7%)** with no change to other sterols — proof that point mutations re-tune steroid selectivity. 〔src: "Remarkably, we observed a 6-fold decrease in cross-reactivity to cortisol of 5.7%, with no change in cross-reactivity to other tested sterols (Fig. 3d, Supplementary Table 5, Supplementary Fig. 12)."〕 〔src: "Sequencing of the SRTF1_MUT1 gene revealed two mutations located in a region of SRTF1 with the potential to contain the steroid binding domain"〕

## Why it matters to TFsensor
- SRTF1 is a **TetR-family** steroid-responsive aTF — the same family as AcrR — making it a directly relevant precedent for engineering an AcrR steroid biosensor's recognition/allostery. 〔src: "a predicted TetR-family TF"〕
- Demonstrates that **two point mutations in the putative steroid-binding region shift selectivity** (6-fold lower cortisol cross-reactivity, other sterols unchanged) — a template for pocket-design/specificity engineering and for what selectivity gains are achievable from small mutation sets. 〔src: "a 6-fold decrease in cross-reactivity to cortisol of 5.7%, with no change in cross-reactivity to other tested sterols"〕
- Quantifies discrimination among closely related steroids (progesterone vs cortisol/aldosterone vs estrogens vs pregnanediol) — a useful selectivity benchmark/scoring reference for pocket designs against the same steroid panel. 〔src: "This change in DNA binding was highly speciﬁc to progesterone."〕
- Shows DNA-site affinity can be tuned (mutating the operator half-site) to modulate overall sensor dynamic range independent of the ligand pocket. 〔src: "altering the afﬁnity of the aTF to the oligo would modulate overall sensor sensitivity"〕

## Applicability caveats
- TF is **SRTF1 from *Pimelobacter simplex*** (a TetR-family relative, not AcrR itself); sequence/pocket details may differ from AcrR. 〔src: "One steroid metabolizing bacterium is Pimelobacter simplex"〕
- Specificity/affinity were measured by **in vitro BLI and a QD-TF-FRET optical assay** (purified protein + DNA), not in-cell transcriptional readout for the biosensor characterization — transfer to a cellular AcrR sensor may differ. 〔src: "We quantitatively conﬁrmed that SRTF1 is a sterol responsive aTF using biolayer interferometry (BLI)"〕
- Ligand is **progesterone** (a 3-keto-Δ4 steroid); AcrR's native effectors and target steroids may engage the pocket differently.
- No crystal/structural data on the SRTF1 binding pocket; the "steroid binding domain" is only inferred from mutation location. 〔src: "a region of SRTF1 with the potential to contain the steroid binding domain"〕
- Mutant data are preliminary — based on 10 randomly selected mutants, one hit. 〔src: "based on a randomly selected handful of mutants, these results suggest that directed evolution can further improve"〕
